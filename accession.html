MSS. Hale-Marsh Collection · Accepted for deposit 14 March 1924 · Catalogue reference MS. Hale-Marsh I–X with Supplementary Boxes A–C · Condition reports and restrictions noted · Marginal annotations by E. Voss, 2024

The register reproduced below is the Bodleian's formal accession record for the Hale-Marsh papers, as submitted on deposit in March 1924 by the trustees of the estate of Miss Ruth Hale-Marsh. The record is in three hands: a principal cataloguing hand (Bodleian staff, 1924), a secondary hand adding condition notes (believed to be the same cataloguer, 1924–1928), and a third hand adding pencil annotations in the margins (Eleanor Voss, 2024). The pencil annotations are reproduced here in full.

MSS. Hale-Marsh Collection
Papers of the Hale Family of Halecroft, Worcestershire, and the Marsh-Hale Family of Harlow, Essex · circa 1072–1924 · Donated in perpetuity
MS. Hale-Marsh I
Boxes 1–2
c. 1072–1400
The Norman and Medieval Papers
Manorial rolls · parish register fragments · inquest records · Latin writs · 2 boxes · approximately 340 items
Includes the earliest surviving document in the family archive: a land grant notation, in Latin, dated approximately 1072, recording the tenure of one Aldric at Halecroft under the new Norman administration. Also includes the Thomas Hale inquisition material (1351), the Worcester Assizes depositions (1351), and Thomas Hale's Remembrance (1382). The Remembrance is the only document in this volume in English.
Condition: Fair
The 1072 land notation is not, as the 1924 cataloguer believed, a grant. It is a confiscation notice. The distinction is significant. The notation records that Aldric dedit the land — gave it — which in this administrative context means it was taken. The family kept this document for 852 years without apparently recognising what it said about how the family began. Or perhaps they did recognise it and kept it anyway. I find I cannot decide which interpretation I prefer. — E.V., January 2024
Received 1924
MS. Hale-Marsh II
Box 3
c. 1400–1570
The Tudor Papers
Estate accounts · correspondence · household inventories · 1 box · approximately 180 items
Principally account books and household inventories from the period of the Hale family's greatest expansion, including the acquisition of outlying farmland in 1487 and the rebuilding of the hall in 1521. Two items are flagged by the depositors as sensitive: a letter of 1535 that appears to concern an approach made to the family during the dissolution of the monasteries, and a second letter that is a response. The response is in a different hand and is unsigned. The editors note that neither letter is incriminating by the standards of any century other than the sixteenth.
Condition: Good
The dissolution letter was, I think, a near thing for the family. They were approached, they equivocated, and they apparently did nothing either way — which is to say, they held what they had and kept their heads down and survived, which is what this family has always done. The unsigned response says, in effect: we are grateful for the enquiry and will give the matter our full consideration. They gave it no consideration whatsoever, as far as I can tell. Wisely. — E.V., January 2024
Received 1924
MS. Hale-Marsh III
Boxes 4–5
c. 1620–1680
The Civil War and Interregnum Papers
Military correspondence · estate sequestration records · legal documents · 2 boxes · approximately 290 items
Includes the letter informing Eleanor Hale of her husband Thomas's death at Edgehill (1642); the sequestration order of 1646 and the fine payment receipt of 1648; Eleanor Hale's correspondence with the estate trustees during the Interregnum (fourteen letters, all in her hand); and the restoration of tenure documents (1660). Eleanor Hale's letters are the most substantive correspondence in the entire archive prior to the Victorian period.
Condition: Fair — moisture damage to box 5
Eleanor Hale's letters to the trustees are extraordinary. She is managing a sequestered estate, negotiating with a Parliamentary committee, raising a son alone, and she writes with a precision and a steadiness that I find remarkable for any period. The letter of November 1650 — in which she corrects a mathematical error in the sequestration calculation and asks, politely, that the committee reconsider the figure — is marked in the margin in pencil, presumably by George Hale cataloguing in 1858: "Grandmother's letter — she was right, they revised it." Yes. She was. — E.V., January 2024
Received 1924
MS. Hale-Marsh IV
Box 6
c. 1710–1745
The Georgian Papers
Estate accounts · correspondence · personal letters · 1 box · approximately 140 items
A comparatively thin volume by the standards of the collection, reflecting a period of relative stability in the family's fortunes. The principal figure is Richard Hale (1700–1762), who managed the estate from his elder brother Charles's accession in 1741 without any formal title to the property. The correspondence between Richard and Charles is notable for its careful maintenance of deference on Richard's side and indifference on Charles's.
Condition: Good
Richard Hale wrote to his brother Charles approximately three hundred letters between 1741 and 1758. Charles appears to have replied to approximately twelve of them, mostly to request money. Richard's letter of June 1749 — in which he explains, in five patient paragraphs, that the estate cannot support the expenditure Charles has proposed, that the estate has not been able to support this expenditure for the past seven years, and that he, Richard, would be grateful for some acknowledgement that Charles has received at least some of the previous letters on this subject — is, I think, a masterpiece of restrained fury. He signs it, as always: Your obedient brother, R. — E.V., February 2024
Received 1924
MS. Hale-Marsh V
Boxes 7–8
c. 1795–1860
The Regency and Early Victorian Papers
Estate accounts · personal correspondence · diaries · 2 boxes · approximately 420 items
The richest single volume of the collection, principally on account of the papers of Augusta Hale (1800–1858). Includes her correspondence (over two hundred letters), her household accounts (meticulously maintained from 1823 to 1857), and her diary fragments (four volumes, partial, 1818–1855). The diary volumes are flagged as restricted; see restriction notice below.
Condition: Fair — diary vol. II water-stained
Augusta's accounts are kept in double-entry, which she apparently taught herself from a book. The handwriting in the accounts is noticeably different from the handwriting in her letters — smaller, more controlled, more careful — as if she understood that the accounts were a different kind of document and required a different kind of self. I think she was right about that. I also think it must have been exhausting to be Augusta Hale, and that the record does not fully account for this. — E.V., February 2024
Received 1924
MS. Hale-Marsh VI
Box 9
c. 1860–1884
The Late Victorian Papers — The Edmund Hale Material
Correspondence · legal documents · deed-box contents · 1 box + 1 deed-box · approximately 95 items
Contains the papers of Edmund Hale (1841–1882), the last of the Hale line in the main branch. Includes personal correspondence, estate documents, and the deed-box, which was deposited sealed. The deed-box contains, inter alia, forty-seven items catalogued under MS. Hale-Marsh VII items 1–47. Two items within the deed-box (items 38 and 39) were catalogued as sealed in 1924 and were not opened until January 2024.
Condition: Deed-box fragile — do not stack
Items 38 and 39 had been sealed for one hundred years. The wax was intact. When I cut it, the paper unfolded with a small sound that I thought was worth noting. I have been in this archive for eleven years. I have opened a great many sealed documents. This one was different, in the sense that I knew — before I read a word — that I was about to find out something that the person who sealed it had decided no one should know. He was wrong that no one should know. But I understand why he thought so. — E.V., January 2024
Received 1924
Supp. A–C
Boxes 10–12
c. 1884–1924
The Hale-Marsh Supplementary Papers — The Transition Period
Legal correspondence · estate transfer documents · Marsh family papers · 3 boxes · approximately 310 items
Material relating to the transfer of the Hale estate to the Hale-Marsh trustees following Edmund Hale's death, and subsequently to the Marsh-Hale family. Includes correspondence between the solicitors and Miss Ruth Hale-Marsh (Edmund's great-niece by the Marsh line), who directed that the papers be deposited at the Bodleian. Ruth's letter of instruction, dated November 1923, requests that the collection be treated as a complete archive — that nothing be discarded, regardless of apparent significance — and that a condition be placed on access to certain items for a period of one hundred years. The restriction lapsed, as directed, on 1 January 2024.
Condition: Good
Ruth Hale-Marsh knew what was in the deed-box. She must have — she was the one who directed the restriction. She chose one hundred years, which would have put her well beyond any living memory of the people involved. She wanted it found, eventually. She just wanted everyone who had been hurt by it to be safely dead first. I think that was a considered decision and possibly a kind one. I have thought about it a great deal. — E.V., March 2024
Received 1924
Restriction Notice · Hale-Marsh Family Trust Instruction · November 1923
The following items are restricted from public access for a period of one hundred years from the date of accession: MS. Hale-Marsh VII items 38, 39 (sealed letters); MS. Hale-Marsh V items 201–204 (Augusta Hale diary fragments, personal passages marked with a red cross in the original). Restriction on items 38–39 lapsed 1 January 2024. Restriction on diary items 201–204 lapsed 1 January 2024. Both restrictions have been lifted and materials are now available to all readers of the archive.
