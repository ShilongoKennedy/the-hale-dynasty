Series Preface
This  volume  contains  the  complete  chronicle  of  the  Hale  family  of  
Worcestershire, assembled in a single edition for the first time. The ten books were  
composed over the course of 2024 and 2025 by James Marsh-Hale and Eleanor Voss,  
working from the Hale family papers deposited at the Bodleian Library, Oxford,  
under the reference MSS. Hale-Marsh.
The collection begins in 1066 with Ranulf de la Hale — a Norman soldier of no  
particular distinction who crossed the Channel with William's army and was given, in  
the  aftermath  of  the  Conquest,  a  small  parcel  of  confiscated  Saxon  land  in  
Worcestershire. It ends in 2026 with two people working in Oxford and London to  
assemble the record of what that land, and the family that held it, became across  
nine centuries.
What connects the ten books is not a continuous narrative — the archive has  
gaps, the documents are of different kinds, the voices change with the centuries —  
but a continuous question. Ranulf asked it on his deathbed in 1115: whether what he  
had done was sufficient. Every Hale after him asked some version of the same  
question. None of them answered it cleanly. All of them kept asking.
The language of each volume is the language of its period. Brother Eadmer's  
monastic English of 1121 is not the same instrument as John Hale's Tudor English of  
1534, which is not the same instrument as Augusta Hale's Regency English of 1822,  
which is not the same instrument as the contemporary English of the emails and  
journal entries that close the chronicle. The reader who moves through all ten  
volumes will experience the whole history of the English language as well as the  
whole history of one family — and will find, perhaps, that the two histories are not  
entirely separable.
The question is the thread. The language is the costume it wears in each century.  
The family is the body that carries both.
A note on structure: each of the ten books contains its own front matter,  
genealogical table, and glossary. These are preserved in this omnibus edition. The  
reader is therefore free to enter the chronicle at any point without losing the  
apparatus of the individual volumes. The series preface and master timeline at the  
front of this edition are provided for those who prefer to read in sequence.
James Marsh-Hale and Eleanor Voss
Oxford and London, March 2026

The Preface of Brother Eadmer
Being an Account of the Sources from which this Chronicle is Drawn
In the name of God the Father, and of His Son our Lord Jesus Christ, and of 
the Holy Spirit, one God in three persons, I Brother Eadmer, a monk of the 
Cathedral Priory of the Blessed Virgin Mary at Worcester, unworthy servant  
and poor sinner, set down here what I have gathered, by diligent inquiry 
and by the testimony of those who saw and heard and did, of the history of 
the family called de la Hale, who hold their lands at Halecroft in the county 
of Worcester by the grant of King William the Conqueror and by the 
subsequent confirmation of his son King William Rufus, and whose present 
lord, Sir Geoffrey de la Hale, has charged me upon his soul and mine to 
preserve what is known before it is lost.
I write in the year 1121, which is to say in the fifty-fifth year since the 
coming of the Normans to England, and in the twenty-first year of the reign 
of our present sovereign lord King Henry, the Conqueror's youngest son. 
Those who lived through the first years of the Conquest are now old, or 
dead, or speaking with the uncertain memory of age, and the prior of this 
house, Father Oswald, has for some years exhorted the monks of this 
community to preserve in writing what old men tell us of those times, before  
the telling stops.
The sources from which I have drawn are these: first, such entries as 
exist in the annals of this house pertaining to events in this county; second, 
the oral testimony of Edwin the Saxon, who is now past seventy years of age  
and who was a young man in the household of the thegn Leofric of Halecroft  
when the Normans came, and who has spoken to me on six separate 
occasions, with a frankness that I attribute to his age and to his evident 
conviction that there is no longer anything to fear from the truth; third, a 
writ of land grant, in Latin, which Sir Geoffrey holds in his charter chest 
and permitted me to read and to copy; fourth, the testimony of Dame Maud 
de la Hale, Sir Geoffrey's mother, who is sixty years of age and whose 
recollections of her father-in-law Ranulf, though coloured by the natural 
piety of a widow, are of considerable historical interest; and fifth, my own 
observation of the de la Hale family and their tenants over many years of 
pastoral acquaintance.
I have endeavoured to set down what is true, and to mark clearly what 
is uncertain, and to omit what cannot be verified. Where the testimony of 
witnesses conflicts, I have said so. Where the record is silent, I have not 
invented. A chronicle that invents is not a chronicle but a sermon, and I 
have had enough experience of sermons to know that they are more 
satisfying to the preacher than to the congregation.
I dedicate this work to the glory of God and to the preservation of 
memory, which is itself, as the blessed Augustine teaches, a form of the 
presence of God in time.
Brother Eadmer of Worcester
The Priory of St Mary, Worcester
Written in the month of October, Anno Domini 1121
I
Of Normandy, and of Ranulf de la Hale, and of  
the Reason He Came to England
Anno Domini 1066
In the duchy of Normandy, in the village of La Hale-sur-Risle in the valley of  
the river Risle, there was born in the year of Our Lord 1040 a boy who was 
given the name Ranulf, being the third son of a man called Godfrey who 
held half a hide of land in that valley by service to his lord, and who was 
himself the son of a Northman — that is to say, a man of the Viking blood 
that had settled in Normandy these hundred and thirty years past and had, 
in that time, learned the French tongue and the French manner of fighting 
but had not, his grandson would later say, entirely relinquished the Viking 
manner of wanting.
The village of La Hale takes its name from the old word for an 
enclosure, a hall, a place set apart — hale in the Norman tongue, which is 
the same word in its root as the Saxon heall that the English used for the 
great hall of their lords, so that when Ranulf de la Hale arrived in England 
and found that the English called such places by a name so close to his own,  
he took it as a sign, which may tell us something about the man that the 
rest of this chronicle will either confirm or complicate.
He was the third son. In the world of 1066, this was a condition of 
singular clarity: the eldest would have the land, the second would have the 
Church or a senior position in a lord's household if fortune served, and the 
third would have what he could take. Ranulf was not, by the testimony of 
those who knew him, a man of resignation. He was, in the phrase that 
Edwin the Saxon used when he described him to me — and Edwin used it 
with a directness that suggested he had spent many years arriving at the 
exact word — a man of appetite. He wanted. He wanted land, and a name 
attached to land, and sons to carry the name, and the particular security 
that only the possession of ground beneath one's feet can confer upon a 
man who has grown up watching others possess it.
It was this appetite that brought him, in the autumn of 1066, across the 
Channel in the fleet of William, Duke of Normandy, who had his own 
appetite, considerably larger, and the military and political genius to satisfy 
it.
    ✦ ✦ ✦
What we know of Ranulf's early life is sparse. He was, by the time of the  
crossing, twenty-five or twenty-six years of age. He had served in William's 
household forces for at least four years — Edwin, who heard this from the 
man himself, says six — and had distinguished himself sufficiently at the 
siege of Domfront in 1063 to receive a small payment and the personal 
commendation of the duke, which was, in practical terms, the currency 
most likely to produce future reward. He was not a knight. He was a man-
at-arms, which is to say a mounted soldier of some skill but without the 
formal investiture of knighthood and without the lands that knighthood 
required. He was, in the hierarchy of William's army, a man of the middle —  
above the foot soldiers, below the lords, and in precisely the condition of 
motivated ambition that made such men useful to a duke who needed 
fighters willing to cross open water in autumn to take a kingdom.
The crossing was made in September of the year 1066. The fleet 
numbered, by the accounts that survive, somewhere between four hundred 
and seven hundred ships — the chroniclers disagree, as chroniclers always 
disagree about numbers, in direct proportion to the impressiveness of the 
numbers in question. What is not disputed is that it was the largest military 
crossing of the Channel since the Romans, and that the men who made it 
knew, as they looked back at the receding coast of Normandy, that they 
were either going to England or to the bottom of the sea.
[Note by the scribe, Brother Eadmer:]
I have asked Edwin whether Ranulf spoke of 
the crossing to him. He said: "Often. He said 
it was the longest night of his life and he had 
lived through several long nights. He said the 
horses were worse off than the men, being 
unable to understand what was happening to 
them, and that he spent much of the crossing 
steadying his horse by speaking into its ear, 
which is how he learned that comfort offered 
to another creature steadies the offerer as 
much as the receiver. He said he did not 
sleep." — Brother Eadmer.
II
Of the Battle of Hastings, Being the Account of  
Edwin the Saxon Who Saw It from the English  
Side
The fourteenth day of October, Anno Domini 1066
What follows is the testimony of Edwin, son of Godwin, formerly of the 
household of the thegn Leofric of Halecroft, as given by him to Brother 
Eadmer of Worcester in the Priory of St Mary in the years 1119 and 1120. I 
have set it down as nearly as possible in his own words, though I have 
translated from the English tongue into Latin and here back again, and 
some loss is inevitable in any such passage. I note, for the reader's benefit, 
that Edwin is a man of remarkable lucidity for his age, and that his account 
of the battle is consistent, across three separate tellings, in all material 
particulars.
— Brother Eadmer.
    ✦ ✦ ✦
"I was seventeen years old in the year the Normans came. My father was a 
free man, a ceorlisc man as we say, who held two virgates of land from the 
thegn Leofric at Halecroft, and I was in Leofric's service as a household 
man, which meant I carried messages and kept accounts and did whatever 
was asked of me, and when Leofric rode to war I rode with him. This is how 
I came to be at the great battle."
"The English army had already fought at Stamford Bridge in the north, 
against the Norwegian king Harald Hardrada, and the English king Harold 
had beaten the Norwegians so thoroughly that very few of them went home,  
and Harald Hardrada was among those who did not. It was a great victory. 
But the news came south within days that the Normans had landed on the 
southern coast, and so King Harold, who was a man of absolute 
determination and perhaps insufficient patience, turned his army and 
marched it south again before the men had properly rested from the 
northern battle. Leofric said to my father, the night before we left: 'We are 
tired. Harold should rest us. But Harold is Harold, and we march.'"
"The battle was on the fourteenth day of October, on a ridge 
called Senlac, which the English call the hoary apple tree hill. The 
Normans came up the slope toward us. We had the high ground, 
which should have been an advantage, and in the morning it was. 
The Norman knights came up the hill and we held the shield wall 
and they broke on it like water on a cliff face. I was in the middle 
of the line with Leofric's men and I could feel the wall through my 
own shield, the pressure of a thousand men all leaning into it at 
once, and the weight of the horses and the spearmen coming 
against it, and for a time the wall held and the Normans fell back 
down the slope."
"I do not know at what hour the shield wall broke. Time in a 
battle is not the time of ordinary life; it stretches and collapses and  
you cannot hold it in your mind. What I know is that at some point 
in the afternoon the Normans pretended to retreat, and some of 
our men — not Leofric's, Leofric kept us firm — broke out of the 
wall to pursue them, and when they pursued, the Normans turned 
and killed them, and the wall was broken, and after that it was a 
different kind of battle."
"The king died in the late afternoon. I did not see it. I heard it 
pass down the line like a wave, the word that Harold was dead, 
and then the line began to come apart, and the dying really began.  
Leofric took an axe-blow to the shoulder in that last hour and did 
not die of it then but died of the wound three weeks later, in great 
pain, which was a worse death than Harold's. I carried him off the 
field on my back. He was not a light man."
"I saw the Normans that day. Not as men, then — they were 
the enemy, they were the killing thing, they were what you tried 
not to die from. It was later that they became men to me. One of 
them in particular."
"After Leofric died I went home to Halecroft, because I had nowhere 
else to go and my father was there. William's army was moving through the 
south and midlands, taking everything, burning what resisted and taking 
what did not. We heard stories. A village burned here, a church robbed 
there, a thegn killed for not submitting fast enough. Leofric's sons had fled 
north. The land at Halecroft had no lord. It was waiting to be given to 
someone, and we all knew it, and knowing it was a particular kind of fear — 
not the fear of a thing happening but the fear of waiting for a thing to 
happen."
"The Norman came in the spring of 1067. He came with two other 
soldiers and a clerk who carried a document, and he rode into the yard at 
Halecroft and looked at the house and the fields and the mill and the wood, 
and my father and the other free men stood in the yard and looked at him, 
and he looked back at us, and then the clerk read the document, which was 
in Latin and which none of us could understand, and the Norman said 
something to the clerk and the clerk said to us, in English that was not quite  
English: 'This man is Ranulf de la Hale. This land is his, by the king's gift. 
You serve him now.'"
"That is how it was done. That is how England changed hands."
III
The Writ of Grant, and of What It Gave and  
What It Did Not Give
Anno Domini 1067
Among the documents preserved in Sir Geoffrey de la Hale's charter chest, 
which he permitted me to examine in the year 1119, is a writ of land grant, 
written in Latin on vellum, much worn at the fold-lines but legible 
throughout. I have copied it here, as exactly as the condition of the 
document permitted, together with my translation into the English tongue.
— Brother Eadmer.
Writ of King William I, Granting Lands at Halecroft
Issued at Winchester, in the year of Our Lord 1067
[In the original Latin:]
Willelmus rex Anglorum omnibus fidelibus suis Francis et Anglis in 
Wigornscira salutem.
Sciatis me dedisse et concessisse Ranulfo de Hala, militi meo, pro 
servitio suo fideli ad Hastingas et alibi, terram que vocatur Halecroft in 
comitatu Wigorniensi, que fuit Leofric thegni, qui contra me pugnavit et 
mortuus est.
Tenebit ipse Ranulfus dictam terram et omnes pertinencias suas sibi et 
heredibus suis de me et heredibus meis per servitium unius militis in 
perpetuum.
Quare volo et firmiter precipio quod ipse et heredes sui bene et in pace 
et honorifice teneant predictam terram cum omnibus libertatibus et 
liberis consuetudinibus suis.
Testibus: Roberto Comite Moritonii. Willelmo Episcopo Londoniensi. 
Datum apud Wintoniam.
[Translation:]
William, King of the English, to all his faithful men, French and 
English, in Worcestershire, greeting.
Know that I have given and granted to Ranulf de la Hale, my soldier, 
for his faithful service at Hastings and elsewhere, the land called 
Halecroft in the county of Worcester, which was the land of the thegn 
Leofric, who fought against me and is dead.
The said Ranulf shall hold the said land and all its appurtenances, to 
himself and his heirs, from me and my heirs, by the service of one 
knight, in perpetuity.
Wherefore I will and firmly command that he and his heirs hold the 
aforesaid land well and in peace and honourably, with all its liberties 
and free customs.
Witnessed by: Robert, Count of Mortain. William, Bishop of London. 
Given at Winchester.
[Note by the scribe, Brother Eadmer:]
This document is authentic, as I judge by the 
form of the Latin, the witnesses named, and 
the condition of the seal, which though 
broken retains the impression of King 
William's equestrian figure. I note that the 
grant is made "for faithful service at Hastings 
and elsewhere" — the elsewhere is not 
specified, but Dame Maud tells me her father-
in-law Ranulf said it referred to the harrying 
of certain villages in Kent that resisted the 
Norman advance in the weeks after Hastings. 
He did not speak of this with pride, she said, 
but he did not speak of it with shame either. 
He spoke of it as a soldier speaks of what he 
has done — which is to say, as something that  
happened, and that produced a consequence, 
and that is now over. — Brother Eadmer.
The writ gave Ranulf de la Hale the land. What it did not give him — what 
no writ could give — was the understanding of what he had received.
Halecroft was not empty land. It was a working estate of some two 
hundred and forty acres, comprising arable strips in the open field, a water-
mill on the brook that ran along the eastern boundary, a wood of perhaps 
thirty acres used for pannage and timber, and a settlement of eight families 
— four free men, of whom Edwin's father was one, and four villeins who 
held their strips in return for labour services. There was a hall, which the 
Saxons had built of timber and which would have been, by Norman 
standards, a considerable building of its kind. There were cattle, sheep, 
pigs, and geese. There was a granary that was, in the spring of 1067, still 
partly stocked with the previous year's harvest.
All of this was Ranulf's. But none of it spoke French. None of it had 
fought at Hastings or anywhere else in his service. None of it knew who he 
was or what the writ in the clerk's hand said or why it should mean 
anything to them. What they knew was that Leofric was dead and that this 
Norman was standing in the yard, and that between those two facts their 
world had changed in a way they had not chosen and could not undo.
Ranulf knew this too. Edwin told me — and this is the detail that has 
stayed with me most persistently in the years since Edwin told it to me — 
that when the clerk finished reading the writ, and the yard was silent, and 
the English stood in a row looking at him, Ranulf dismounted. He did not 
have to. A Norman lord arriving to take possession of confiscated English 
land had no requirement to dismount, no protocol that insisted upon it. He 
dismounted because, Edwin said, he was a man who understood that a man 
on horseback looking down at men on foot is not in a position to begin 
anything that requires the cooperation of those men.
He stood in the yard. He looked at them. He said something in French. 
The clerk translated: he said that he had no intention of burning anything 
that was still standing, and that he required food and lodging for himself 
and his men, and that he would speak with the heads of each household on 
the following day, one at a time.
Edwin's father said, in English, that food would be found and that the 
hall was available. The clerk translated. Ranulf nodded. He walked to the 
hall and went inside.
"That evening," Edwin told me, "my father said: he is a practical man. I 
do not know yet whether that is better or worse than the other kind. We will  
find out." My father was right on both counts. — Edwin's testimony, as 
recorded by Brother Eadmer."
