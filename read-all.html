Chronicle of Halecroft · Brother Eadmer · 1121

In the year of Our Lord one thousand and sixty-six, in the month of October, a man came to this valley who had not been here before. He was a Norman, one of the Conqueror's men, and he had been given the land by a king who had not asked the land's opinion. His name was Ranulf, and he was, by the accounts of those who had reason to fear him and those who had reason to be grateful, a man of considerable practical intelligence and very little patience.

He looked at the valley. He looked at what was left of the Saxon holding. He said something in French that no one understood. Then he stayed.

— Brother Eadmer, Priory of Pershore, 1121

William Hale III · Letter to his steward · 1534

The King has dissolved the monastery at Pershore and taken the bells. I do not know what to make of this. I am told not to know what to make of this. I am learning, at some cost, that there are seasons in which the most prudent philosophy is to have very few opinions and to express them very quietly.

— William Hale III, 1534

Thomas Hale · Final letter to his brother · September 1651

James — if you find this after, know that I thought of you at Powick Bridge and did not fire. I do not know if you extended me the same courtesy. I suspect you did. We are, neither of us, very good at this. I am sorry for all of it. Give my love to the land. I think the land will still be there when the two of us have sorted ourselves out.

— Thomas Hale, Worcester, September 1651

Augusta Hale · Letter to her brother Edmund · March 1814

You ask whether I am content. I am content in the way that a person who has been given one small room in a very large house is content: I have made excellent use of the room, I have hung it beautifully, and I am not going to tell you what I think of the arrangement because you would find it ungrateful. I am not ungrateful. I am simply very clear-eyed about the size of the room.

— Augusta Hale, March 1814

Edmund Hale · Confession document · undated · c.1882

I have been performing Edmund Hale for forty-four years and I am very tired. The performance has been, by most measures, excellent. The reviews have been good. No one has noticed that it is a performance, which is either a tribute to my skill or an indictment of the audience, and I no longer have the energy to determine which.

I am not what anyone thinks I am. I have not been, for a very long time. I am leaving this here because I need someone to know, and there is no one I can tell.

— Edmund Hale, c.1882

Thomas Marsh-Hale · Letter home · August 1916

I have been thinking about the house. Not with any particular plan — I don't have any particular plans at the moment, which seems prudent — but just the smell of it, and the way the light came through the kitchen window in the morning. I find myself thinking about very specific things. I don't know what that means. I think it probably means something.

— Thomas Marsh-Hale, France, August 1916

Edmund Marsh-Hale · Personal memo · October 1940

House at Clapham: destroyed. Contents: mostly gone. The family box was in the Anderson shelter. This is the only reason I am still in possession of nine hundred years of paper. I find this either very funny or very significant and I am not in the right state to determine which. Keeping the box. Keeping asking.

— Edmund Marsh-Hale, October 1940

Eleanor Voss · Email to James Marsh-Hale · 15 January 2024

Mr. Marsh-Hale — the imaging results are back. The central passage of the letter is legible. I have read it several times. I need to speak to you as soon as possible. She was writing to someone. I think she was writing to us. I think she knew that someone would eventually come looking, and she left enough of a trail to be found.

She was right. We found her.

— Eleanor Voss, Bodleian Library, 15 January 2024
